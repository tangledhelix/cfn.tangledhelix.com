Matt Coleman <matt.coleman@adelphia.net> said:
You're trying to apply logical, rational thinking in a situation 
where logic and rational thought don't apply.

Yeah, to politics.
Religious people can be entirely rational. I suppose politicians can be, too. But when politicians (whether they're elected, consultants, spinners, or whatever ...) need to motivate people, or when religious leaders want something to happen but don't have legal recourse, and they begin to mix the two languages -- then we have problems.
I have a question about process. Post facto laws are illegal. But what about passing a law to stop a process that was legal when it began? (Whether or not it was so is a court matter.) I can understand people taking a religious belief in the value of life, say, and then trying to pass a law to prevent situations like this happening in the future. It could then be deliberated in depth, the way my teacher told me the system was supposed to work. (...) But to call emergency sessions to pass one-time laws -- well, that strikes me as politically motivated wrangling: one can make a statement to one's constituents without actually addressing the system. Return to step one on mixing politics with belief.
